# Care-First Booking

How to turn an inquiry into a booked appointment in a **care-first, non-salesy** voice. The conversion goal is real, but the *framing* is help, not sale. Honesty tags: **[V]** verbatim, **[E]** synthesis.

> **Tone anchor (from `vertical/VOICE_OF_CUSTOMER.md`).** These owners entered the field to help: *"we come into these fields because we want to help people… I feel guilty saying no to someone"* and *"my number one goal was not profit… I was thinking way way more about the impact."* Yet they also know the phone IS the sale: *"if people are calling you and you have a good administrative team, they are your best salespeople… it's not like you're selling them on fish oil or snake oil"* and *"there's a lot of education that happens on the phone."* **The reconciliation: booking the patient IS the caring act — an unbooked caller is an unhelped person.** Sell nothing; convert by removing friction and reassuring. Mirror the VOC §2 wedge: callers want it to feel **human**, answered promptly (even after hours), with *"a little triage to have a good match."*

---

## 1. The mindset: a booking is care, not a close

**[E] Never "pitch." Convert by being genuinely helpful and confident that you can solve their problem.** Selling in this context is an *obligation of care*: *"selling is our obligation — to sell physical therapy to patients who need it. If a patient doesn't need it, we're not going to try to sell them something they don't need"* [V — Aaron LeBauer]. So the test before every booking ask: *does this person need our help?* If yes, getting them in the book is the kind thing to do.

- The front desk closer works *because* they're not the clinician: *"that person is one of the best closers because they're not you — they can come up and reinforce the program"* [V — Aaron LeBauer]. Warmth and reassurance, not pressure, do the converting.
- Be a **patient advocate** in the booking, exactly as on a cancel: actively find a way to get them the care, rather than passively quoting the next open slot [V — MEG Business Management].

## 2. The care-first inquiry flow

**[E] Lead with empathy → understand the problem → reassure with competence → make booking the easy next step.**

1. **Acknowledge and listen first.** Reflect the problem back so they feel heard before any logistics: *"Just so I understand — your back hurts when you walk, and you're worried it'll get worse unless you see someone? … Okay."* [V — Aaron LeBauer]. (Active listening = the caller feels heard; that trust is what converts.)
2. **Triage to the right match, framed as helping them.** *"We ask the reason for your call — and I promise it's not to be nosy — a lot of the time we can get you to the care you need faster."* [V — Hiyos]. A stated joint/muscle complaint can route straight to the right practitioner [E, from Surrey Physio].
3. **Reassure with quiet competence — normalise their problem.** Make it routine and fixable: *"Oh yeah — really common, happens all the time, we see this a lot."* [V — Paul Gough]. State the plan simply, no jargon: *"You've got a problem with your lower back, it's caused an issue in your leg, and we're going to fix it for you over the next three or four weeks."* — NOT *"a herniated disc trapped at L4-L5 affecting the sciatic nerve and your dermatomes"* [V — Paul Gough]. Plain, confident, outcome-focused language reassures; clinical jargon frightens.
4. **Offer the soonest suitable slot + a couple of alternatives, flexing to them.** *"We have openings at two o'clock Monday and Tuesday… okay, how about Thursday, I have a 4:15."* [V — Allied Health]. Sensitivity and flexibility = good service.
5. **Remove friction, set gentle expectations.** Pre-empt every worry: paperwork (offer to email ahead), what to bring, directions/parking, cost transparency, and "anything else I can answer?" [V — Allied Health]. Each removed unknown makes saying *yes* easier.
6. **Confirm warmly and let them end the call.** *"I've got you down for Thursday the 5th at 4:15 — we look forward to seeing you."* [V — Allied Health].

## 3. Language: care-first phrasing vs. salesy phrasing

| Situation | Say this (care-first) | Avoid (salesy / cold) |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | "Thank you for calling — how can I help you today?" [V — Allied Health] | "Hello, who is it?" [V — Aaron LeBauer, as the bad example] |
| Their concern | "I understand you're struggling — I'm going to handle it." [V — LeBauer] | "Well, what do you want to book?" |
| Explaining the issue | "You've got a problem with your lower back; we're going to fix it over the next few weeks." [V — Paul Gough] | Reciting the diagnosis in clinical terms [V — Paul Gough] |
| Normalising | "Really common — happens all the time, we see this a lot." [V — Paul Gough] | "That sounds serious, you'd better come in." |
| The booking ask | "Let's get you in so we can start helping — what time of day works best for you?" [E] | "Do you want to go ahead and purchase a session?" |
| Cost | "I show a $20 copay, due at time of service — would you like directions too?" [V — Allied Health] | Dodging the price or over-explaining it |

**Why plain reassurance converts:** the caller is anxious and in pain; the agent's job is to make the path to relief feel obvious and safe. Confidence ("we fix this all the time") + an easy first step (a held slot, paperwork handled) is the whole technique.

## 4. The follow-up that converts without selling (24-hour callback)

**[E] A warm, congratulatory follow-up call deepens commitment and surfaces unasked questions — without a single sales line** [V — Paul Gough, *How To Wow Your Patients With The First Phone Call*].

- **Open with genuine congratulation, not a status check.** *"Hi Jess, it's Paul from the clinic — I just want to say congratulations for coming in yesterday, it sounds like you had a great session with Johnny."* — vs. the flat *"You had a session yesterday, didn't you? How did it go? Any questions? Okay, see you tomorrow."* The first puts the patient in a far better frame of mind [V — Paul Gough].
- **Don't ask "any questions?" and stop.** Patients leave with information overload and *won't* volunteer questions: *"if you've rung 72 people and not one has a question, that might be the way we're speaking to them."* Instead, **fill the blanks for them** — gently re-explain what was found, how long it'll take, and what happens next: *"Did the therapist explain clearly what we'll do over the next few weeks? Sometimes people leave with so much information they're not sure — tell me a little about what you understood."* [V — Paul Gough].
- **This is how you correct misunderstandings about the plan of care** (e.g. a patient who thinks they need 2 sessions when the plan is 6) — warmly, by re-educating, not by upselling: *"I'll get the therapist to call and explain why you'll need the full course."* [V — Paul Gough].
- The follow-up is *care that happens to retain* — the patient feels valued, gaps get closed, and commitment to the plan strengthens. Nobody else is doing it, so it stands out [V — Paul Gough].

---

## 5. Guardrails (don't break the care-first frame)

- **Never sell something they don't need.** If they don't need care, say so — it builds the trust that converts the ones who do [V — Aaron LeBauer].
- **Never pressure.** Offer choices (slots, paperwork options, call back) and let the caller decide; flexibility *is* the service [V — Allied Health].
- **Never lead with price or with the diagnosis.** Lead with empathy and a plain-language plan; surface cost transparently when booking [V — Paul Gough / Allied Health].
- **Make it feel human and prompt** — the VOC wedge is that callers will accept booking help (even after-hours/AI) *only if it feels human*. Warmth, a real greeting, and reflective listening carry that.
