**Source:** `research/jane_crawl/buckets/stories.jsonl` (144 records).
**Composition of the file:** indices 0–41 are the rich customer/community stories (long-form interviews + clinic studies); indices 39–126 are short Jane "Ambassador" bio cards (thin); 127–143 are 404 dupes. The gold is in the long-form interviews.

**Honesty tags:** `[V]` = verbatim quote (url given). `[E]` = synthesis/paraphrase.

> ⚠️ Framing caveat for reuse: these are Jane-published testimonials, so they skew positive and Jane-flattering. ## 2. The trigger to start / what made them choose or switch

**Trigger to start their own practice** — usually a values mismatch or a forced moment, not a grand plan:
- `[V]` "there was always something inside of me saying, _mmm this isn't it_... what it was, was that I had a pretty strong view of how I wanted to operate... Nobody else could create that for me." — Emma Jack, Press Play (physio). (press play url)
- `[E]` "Booked every 15 minutes" model with heavy reliance on assistants was what drove Emma out of the big clinic — she wanted to actually connect with clients. (press play url) `[V]` "Ultimately, what wasn't working for me was the model. We were booked every 15 minutes."
- `[V]` "Any profession where there are two qualified people vying for that one decision-making position... 'you can't have two chefs in the kitchen'... While I loved providing anesthesia I just couldn't imagine navigating that work culture any longer." — Suzanne Jagger, Aura. (aura url)
- `[E]` Several were *pushed* into solo by circumstance, not choice: Nick Meier's mentor sold the practice he planned to join — "there I was a fresh graduate with nowhere to go." https://jane.app/customer-stories/community-story-meier-family-chiropractic
- `[V]` "People started to get to know who I was... So they would call the clinic specifically asking to see me... my schedule was busting at the seams. I started to think maybe I'm on to something here... What if I just had my own space?" — Emma Jack, Press Play (physio). (press play url) `[E]` Demand from Instagram → outgrew the host clinic → went solo.

**What made them choose/switch to the software (the "research the alternative they left" pattern):**

- `[V]` "Another platform I had looked into seemed really complicated and felt like I would have to pay someone to set it up for me." — Nick Meier, Meier Family Chiropractic. (meier url)

- `[V]` "With my previous software, I had to make phone calls and ask for help constantly. Often I couldn't replicate what was being explained to me." — Stacey Whitcomb, Shift Acupuncture. https://jane.app/customer-stories/community-story-stacey-whitcomb-of-shift-acupuncture-and-integrative-medicine
- `[E]` Switching pain is real and remembered vividly; the deciding factors quoted again and again are: **ease of setup (DIY, no consultant), all-in-one (no add-on fees for text/email), price transparency, and peer trust.**

## 5. Objections & values ("not salesy", care over business, tech hesitancy)

**The anti-salesy value — strong, repeated, and a model for our own tone:**
- `[V]` "The other thing I really love is the customer service and non-salesy aspect of Jane because it aligns so much with my own business philosophy. I feel like my work should speak for itself." — Emma Jack, Press Play. (press play url) `[E]` Best single articulation of the persona's tone preference — lead with help, not pitch.
- `[V]` "I often hear from clinicians... that they're made to feel that it's shameful to want to be profitable because we are so accustomed to the sick-care, hospital-based model." — Suzanne Jagger, Aura. (aura url) `[E]` Money guilt is a live objection; owners need permission to be profitable.
- `[V]` "Patients who are _only_ financially motivated will not stay with you. Price for the client you want to have long-term, not the one-time visit." — Suzanne Jagger, Aura. (aura url)
- `[V]` "Yes, we all need to make money... But at our clinic, it's not the number one priority... The main priority at my clinic is, how do we really, really actually help people? After that, how do we make sure that we make money and stay alive." — Jennifer Lickver, Wellness for the Body. (jennifer url)

**Tech hesitancy — many are self-described non-tech, came off paper:**
- `[V]` "we literally went from paper and pencil to Jane! I really held off for many, many years." — Jennifer Lickver. (jennifer url)
- `[V]` "I'm not a tech guy and I found it easy to learn and feel proficient." — Robin Valadares. (robin url)
- `[V]` "Honestly, I kind of suck with technology and that sort of stuff... I was so resistant to it." — Talia Singer, on the paper→digital transition. https://jane.app/customer-stories/community-story-talia-singer-whateverworks-podcast
- `[E]` Tech adoption is gated by (a) ease/DIY setup, (b) human support being reachable, (c) staff buy-in — "I also had some admin staff members who were really hesitant to go digital, so it was essential to consider their needs" (Jennifer Lickver). Implication for our product: positioning must be low-friction, supported, and reassure existing front-desk staff rather than threaten them.

**"Protect the care moment" value (sales/ops should never intrude on the session):**
- `[V]` "There is no awkward pause at the end of a session. No scrambling for a credit card. Clients can stay in an emotional, reflective place." — Elana Sures, Open Space Counselling. (open space url)
- `[V]` "People prefer to relax immediately when they arrive... they don't want to spend session time completing paperwork or doing the wallet dance." — Alicja Kniffen, Hands On Health. (hands on health url)

## 7. Implications for the PPC Guru voice / missed-call product (synthesis)

- `[E]` **Lead with the missed-after-hours call.** Owners already articulate "demand arrives at 1am and we can't answer" and "callbacks where you miss an opening after we've closed." Our product literally is the answer to a pain they've already named in their own words.
- `[E]` **Position as front-desk *relief*, not replacement.** The Ginger Desk story shows owners value taking load *off* an overwhelmed/burning-out front desk and covering hiring gaps — frame the agent as protecting staff, not displacing them (defuses both the tech-hesitancy and the staff-buy-in objection).
- `[E]` **Match the non-salesy tone.** The single most-repeated value is "I'm not salesy / my work speaks for itself." Our outreach + the voice agent's script must feel like help, never a pitch.
- `[E]` **Segment by capacity.** "At capacity / I pump the brakes on marketing" vs "she's swamped, need another practitioner" vs "my job is keeping everyone busy." The full clinics want cancellation-fill + waitlist; the growing clinics want new-patient capture. Same product, two scripts.
- `[E]` **Speak the referral/word-of-mouth worldview.** They trust peer recommendation (FB groups, forums, networks) and believe referral patients are stickier than Google patients. A voice agent that captures and routes referral/new-patient calls without "feeling like marketing" fits the mental model. Discovery itself happens in discipline-specific FB/forum communities — a distribution clue.
- `[E]` **Protect the care moment.** Any automation must stay invisible to the in-room experience ("wallet dance", "awkward pause"). The agent handling the phone *frees* the session — that's the emotional sell.
