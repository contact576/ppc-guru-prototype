## What the columns reveal (the analysis)

- **Distribution/data-fit (16) + Moat (12) are what built every winner.** Every candidate that cleared 70 scored **8–10 on Distribution** *and* **6–9 on Moat** (R3 10/7, P1 10/9, C1 10/8, I3 ~8/6). The losers scored fine on Pain/Demand but collapsed on these two: the realtor CRM (R1) and construction CRM (C4) have **Moat 2–3** because the moat is held by incumbents (Zillow/FUB, Jobber) — the founder's ad-data does not transfer to a CRM. The single repeated lesson across all four verticals: **the attribution/voice layers win because the founder's cross-account ad data IS the moat there; the CRM layers lose because that data is orthogonal to a system-of-record.**

- **Not-a-kill-zone (12) is the cleanest separator of GO vs NO-GO.** Winners sit at **7–9** (immigration CRM I1 = 9, the safest niche; PatientROI/JobROI = 8; Realtor ISA = 8). Every NO-GO was knifed here: **Realtor CRM = 1**, **Construction CRM & Voice = 2**, **physio Voice = 3–4**. A low kill-zone score (platform owns the SoR and bundles the feature free — Zillow/Jobber/Jane) was effectively fatal regardless of how strong the pain was.

- **Winnability (14) killed the commodity-voice and CRM plays specifically.** The three physio/construction voice front-desks and both losing CRMs scored **Winnability 2–5** (reseller flood + Jane/Jobber-native rivals, or incumbent-owned segments). By contrast the *attribution* concepts (PatientROI/JobROI/LeadScore) scored **7–8** because the cross-account benchmark is un-clonable. Immigration Voice (I3, Winnability 9) is the outlier winner — it's a voice product that wins on *vertical depth* where the others are horizontal commodities.

- **Pain and Demand barely separated anything — they were necessary, not sufficient.** Almost every candidate scored Pain 7–9 and Demand 6–10, including the dead ones (Construction Voice C5/C6 had the *highest* Pain at 9 and still scored 60). High pain in a kill-zone is a trap, not an opportunity. **Pricing/margin (8)** quietly punished the voice plays (per-minute COGS → 4) and rewarded the software-margin attribution plays (8–9), but at only 8% weight it nudged rather than decided.

**Which candidate died on which parameter (one-liners):**
- **Realtor CRM (39)** — died on **Not-kill-zone (1)** + **Moat (2)**: Zillow owns the lead source, brokerages bundle CRM free.
- **Construction CRM (53)** — died on **Not-kill-zone (2)** + **Moat (3)** + **Winnability (3)**: frontal assault on Jobber, no data transfer.
- **Construction/Physio Voice (54–60)** — died on **Not-kill-zone (2–4)** + **Moat (3)** + **Pricing (4)**: commodity wrappers, platform bundles it, per-minute margin trap.
- **Smart-Reviews (46–47)** — died on **Moat (2)**: pure GPT-wrap, no data edge.
- **Immigration CRM (62)** — died on **Distribution (6)**: 2 clients, no channel, VisaFlo already shipped the hero feature.

## The 3 parameters that most decided outcomes

1. **Distribution/data-fit (16)** — the heaviest weight; every winner scored 8–10, every loser's "win" depended on it. The founder's cross-account ad data is the moat *only* in the attribution/voice-bundle layers.
2. **Not-a-kill-zone (12)** — the cleanest GO/NO-GO line: winners 7–9, every NO-GO scored 1–4 (Zillow / Jobber / Jane owning the system-of-record and bundling the feature).
3. **Moat (12)** + **Winnability (14)** (tie for third) — they move together: the CRM and commodity-voice plays scored 2–5 (incumbent-held moat, reseller-flooded), the attribution plays scored 7–9 (un-clonable cross-account benchmark).

Pain and Demand were uniformly high (7–9) across winners *and* losers — necessary but never decisive.
